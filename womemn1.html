Ellen White's Views on Women Being in the Home and Not Voting

Introduction:

Ellen White, a prominent figure in the Seventh-day Adventist Church, held strong views on the roles of women in society, which were deeply influenced by the cultural and religious context of the 19th century. She believed that a woman's primary duty was in the home, serving as a wife and mother, and nurturing the family both spiritually and morally. Additionally, Ellen White did not advocate for women's suffrage, seeing political engagement as outside the realm of a woman’s God-given responsibilities. Her views were shaped by a conviction that women should maintain their roles within the family, serving as moral and spiritual pillars rather than participating in the political sphere.

Ellen White’s perspective was shared and further developed by other Adventist leaders, including Victor Gill, Joe Crews, Doug Batchelor, and Walter Veith. They have expounded on her teachings, emphasizing the importance of maintaining traditional roles for women to ensure the spiritual health and unity of the family and church.

1. Ellen White's Belief in the Central Role of Women in the Home:

Ellen White consistently emphasized that a woman’s primary sphere of influence was within the home. She believed that women were divinely appointed to manage the household and raise children in the faith. In her view, the home was a sacred institution where women played a vital role in shaping the moral and spiritual character of future generations. White wrote, “The happiness of families and of society depends upon home influences. A woman’s place is in the home, and there she may exert her influence for the highest good" (Testimonies for the Church, Vol. 5, p. 32).

White saw the responsibilities of wife and mother as divinely ordained, suggesting that women who sought roles outside the home were neglecting their God-given duties. “The mother is the queen of her home, and the king on his throne does not have a higher work to perform than she" (The Adventist Home, p. 231). She argued that women’s most important work was to create a loving, nurturing environment that would instill Christian values in their children and support their husbands.

2. The Spiritual Role of Women in the Family:

White believed that women had a unique spiritual role in the family, serving as the primary educators and moral guides for their children. She often referred to the home as the "first school," where children were to learn about God and develop Christian character. “Mothers should educate their children from the cradle to love God and obey His commandments. This is the highest work they are called to do,” she wrote (Child Guidance, p. 18).

Victor Gill, a respected Adventist preacher, has emphasized the significance of Ellen White’s views on the family as a spiritual unit. In his sermons, Gill often highlights that White considered the home to be a place where spiritual development begins. He states, "Ellen White viewed the home as the primary arena for character development and spiritual growth, especially for women, who are called to be teachers of righteousness to their children and to support their husbands in their spiritual leadership" (Victor Gill Sermons Collection, 2015).

3. Women's Role in Supporting the Church Through the Home:

Ellen White extended the importance of women’s roles beyond their immediate family, suggesting that their influence within the home also played a crucial part in the health and growth of the church. She argued that a well-ordered, godly home could serve as a beacon of Christian virtue to the broader community. “The church is composed of families, and if the families are strong in faith, love, and obedience to God, the church will be strong” (The Adventist Home, p. 318).

Joe Crews, another prominent Adventist leader, reinforced this view by explaining that a woman's first and most significant mission field is her home. He stated, “A woman’s influence begins in her home, where she lays the groundwork for the future of the church by raising godly children who will continue the mission" (Amazing Facts Broadcasts, 1982). Crews argued that women have a profound impact on the church's mission and that this influence is most effective when centered within the home.

4. Opposition to Women's Suffrage and Political Engagement:

Ellen White did not support women’s suffrage, believing that women should not be involved in political matters. She viewed the political arena as a domain fraught with moral compromise, deception, and corruption—an environment she considered inappropriate for women. White expressed concerns that political engagement would distract women from their primary duties as wives, mothers, and moral guardians. “It is not a woman’s role to mingle in political strife or to vote. Her work lies in a higher, purer sphere,” she wrote (Fundamentals of Christian Education, p. 182).

Doug Batchelor, in his series on "Women in Prophecy," echoed this perspective, noting that White’s views were consistent with her broader emphasis on maintaining the sanctity of the home. Batchelor stated, “Ellen White believed that women stepping into political roles or seeking the right to vote was a diversion from their higher calling in the home, where they could have the most significant spiritual impact” (Women in Prophecy, 2005).

5. Concerns About Moral Corruption and Political Involvement:

White believed that women’s involvement in politics could lead to moral corruption. She was worried that political life could be incompatible with the Christian values women were meant to uphold. In her writings, she warned that exposure to the contentious and often morally dubious nature of politics could undermine a woman's character and spiritual focus. “Politics is a world of intrigue, strife, and ambition, where a woman can easily lose sight of her high and holy calling,” she cautioned (Testimonies for the Church, Vol. 2, p. 538).

Walter Veith, a modern Adventist scholar, often reflects on Ellen White’s concerns about the corrupting influence of politics, especially on women. In his lecture series "The Bible and the Woman," Veith argues that White’s stance was not an indictment against women’s abilities but rather a concern for their spiritual integrity. “White did not doubt women’s intellect or capabilities, but she feared that the political realm, with its compromises and moral ambiguities, would taint the spiritual purity she believed women should uphold” (Total Onslaught Series, 2010).

6. Women’s Political Involvement Seen as a Threat to Family Stability:

Ellen White was also concerned that political engagement could disrupt the family structure. She argued that if women became preoccupied with political duties, they might neglect their responsibilities at home, leading to instability and discord within the family. “If women leave their homes to engage in public work, the result will be confusion and neglect of home duties,” she warned (The Adventist Home, p. 231).

Victor Gill supports this view by emphasizing that White's concerns were primarily about protecting the sanctity and stability of the family. In his teachings, Gill asserts, “Ellen White believed that when women abandon their God-given roles within the family to seek positions of power or influence outside, the home, the very fabric of Christian society, is threatened” (Victor Gill Sermons Collection, 2015).

7. Ellen White’s Alternative Vision for Women's Influence:

While Ellen White did not support political engagement, she did advocate for women to exert influence in other meaningful ways, particularly within the church and community. She encouraged women to use their talents for teaching, hospitality, charity work, and spreading the gospel. “A woman who fulfills her duty in the home, who raises her children to fear God and supports her husband in his endeavors, exerts a more powerful influence than many who seek public acclaim” (The Adventist Home, p. 243).

Joe Crews also recognized the vital roles women played in the church and community. He believed that White's teachings were not about limiting women but rather directing their energies toward roles where they could make the most significant impact. “Ellen White wanted women to recognize that their influence, especially in teaching and nurturing within the church and community, was invaluable” (Amazing Facts Broadcasts, 1982).

Conclusion:

Ellen White's views on women’s roles were deeply influenced by her time's cultural and religious context. She saw women's primary duties as lying within the home, where they could exert the most significant moral and spiritual influence. White did not support women's suffrage, seeing political engagement as outside a woman’s God-given responsibilities. While her perspectives may appear restrictive by today's standards, they reflect a deep commitment to her faith and her belief in the sanctity of the family unit as the cornerstone of society and the church.

Modern Adventist leaders such as Victor Gill, Joe Crews, Doug Batchelor, and Walter Veith have echoed and expounded upon Ellen White’s views, underscoring the continuing relevance of her teachings in contemporary discussions on gender roles within the church.
