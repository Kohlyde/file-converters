Segment 1: The Elijah Message in Scripture and Prophecy

Introduction: The Role of Elijah in Biblical History

The Elijah message is central to the narrative of both the Old and New Testaments, representing a call to repentance, reform, and a return to true worship. Elijah, the bold prophet of the Old Testament, stands as a model of uncompromising faithfulness to God in times of national apostasy. His confrontations with King Ahab, Queen Jezebel, and the prophets of Baal highlight a period when Israel struggled between two opinions: whether to follow Yahweh or the false gods of their neighbors. Elijah's message was direct, assertive, and unapologetically confrontational�a necessary approach in a time when the truth was clouded by widespread deception and compromise.

In the New Testament, John the Baptist is identified as a fulfillment of the Elijah prophecy, as stated in Malachi 4:5-6: "Behold, I will send you Elijah the prophet before the great and dreadful day of the Lord." John prepared the way for Christ, calling the people to repentance and challenging the religious leaders of his time with uncompromising truth. In the last days, the Bible reveals that another Elijah message will be proclaimed by the 144,000�a group of believers uniquely prepared to deliver a message that "no one else can sing" (Revelation 14:3). This song, this message, is one that is distinctive, assertive, and critical in its call for humanity to make a decisive stand for truth.

The Elijah Message and the End Times

Victor Gill, in his 2015 papers, elucidates that the Elijah message is more relevant today than ever. As we approach the final crisis, the need for a clear, distinct message becomes paramount. Gill argues that the 144,000, like Elijah and John the Baptist, will be bold and unyielding in their proclamation of God's truth. They will not seek to placate or accommodate but will lay out the consequences of choosing compromise over commitment to God.

Gill quotes Revelation 14:6-12, which speaks of the three angels' messages�a warning to the world of impending judgment, a call to worship the Creator, and a stern admonition against receiving the mark of the beast. These messages, according to Gill, are a modern embodiment of the Elijah message, meant to prepare the world for Christ's second coming. The 144,000, he suggests, will be "Elijahs" of the end times, singing a unique song�a song of defiance against falsehood and a declaration of allegiance to God's truth.

The Song No One Else Can Sing

The "song that no one else can sing," referenced in Revelation 14:3, symbolizes an experience unique to the 144,000. It represents a depth of understanding, commitment, and experience that is unparalleled. This song is not merely a literal melody but a narrative, a testimony that comes from living through the final crisis of earth's history and standing firm in faith despite immense opposition. Just as Elijah stood alone on Mount Carmel, declaring, "How long will you waver between two opinions?" (1 Kings 18:21), the 144,000 will stand in a world steeped in confusion and spiritual darkness, singing a song of clarity, conviction, and courage.

Gill points out that the 144,000, like Elijah, will confront the world's religious and political powers with an unflinching message of truth. He emphasizes that their song is "assertive" because it must be; it is not a time for softness or compromise. The world is in a state of moral and spiritual crisis, and the message of Elijah is one that demands a decision�life or death, truth or deception.

The Assertive Nature of Elijah's Ministry

Elijah is often remembered as the "only shit-talking prophet" of the Old Testament, a title earned not through vulgarity but through his unvarnished, bold proclamations against the apostasy of his time. Elijah's confrontation with the prophets of Baal was not a polite debate but a direct challenge to their authority and their gods. "How long will you waver between two opinions?" Elijah demanded, knowing that the people needed to see a clear choice between truth and falsehood (1 Kings 18:21).

Walter Veith, in his commentary, argues that Elijah's approach was precisely what was needed for that time�a moment when compromise was rampant, and the lines between right and wrong were blurred. Veith suggests that God raises up individuals like Elijah, who are prepared to confront the powers of darkness with a fearless message. The "song that nobody else sings" is one born out of a unique experience, a call for a clear decision in a time of spiritual confusion.

Compromise vs. Conviction

Gill and other contemporary theologians such as Stephen Bohr and Doug Batchelor argue that the Elijah message calls for a complete abandonment of compromise. In today's world, compromise is often seen as a virtue�a way to maintain peace and avoid conflict. However, in the context of the Elijah message, compromise is a betrayal of truth. Bohr asserts that Elijah's ministry was a demonstration that there are times when "being nice" is not only ineffective but detrimental to the cause of truth.

Elijah did not seek to make peace with the prophets of Baal; he sought to expose their falsehood and lead the people back to the worship of the true God. Likewise, the 144,000 will not seek to placate or find common ground with those promoting deception. Their mission is to call people to make a clear, unambiguous choice for or against God.

Elijah's Confrontational Approach: A Model for Today

Elijah's confrontational style, as portrayed in the Bible, might seem harsh by modern standards, but it was precisely what was needed to break through the apathy and deception of his time. The prophet's boldness was a reflection of God's own disapproval of Israel's idolatry and moral decline. Gill emphasizes that in the last days, God will raise up people with a similar spirit�people who are willing to speak the truth in love but without watering it down or making it more palatable for a sinful world.

David Asscherick, in his sermons, often refers to Elijah's approach as a necessary "shock to the system." Asscherick argues that the Elijah message is not meant to be comfortable; it is designed to provoke a reaction, to force a decision. This is echoed by Joe Crews, who writes that the Elijah message is a call to action, a challenge to the status quo that demands an immediate and decisive response.

Conclusion: The Relevance of the Elijah Message Today

The Elijah message is not just a relic of biblical history; it is a living, breathing call for our time. The 144,000 will be the Elijahs of the end times, singing a song that no one else can sing because they have an experience no one else has shared. They will be bold, assertive, and uncompromising, like Elijah on Mount Carmel, calling the world to make a choice between truth and deception, life and death, God and the gods of this world.

As Victor Gill concludes in his 2015 papers, "The Elijah message is a song that challenges, convicts, and compels. It is a call to repentance, a call to decision, and ultimately, a call to follow the true God in a world that has largely forgotten Him."

 

 

 

Segment 2: John the Baptist as the New Elijah

Introduction: The Prophetic Role of John the Baptist

John the Baptist stands as a pivotal figure in the New Testament, described by Jesus Himself as the greatest of those born of women (Matthew 11:11). His role was clear: to prepare the way for the coming Messiah, fulfilling the prophecy of Malachi 4:5-6 that Elijah would return before "the great and dreadful day of the Lord." John's ministry, like Elijah's, was marked by a bold and confrontational style, challenging the religious leaders of his time and calling the people to repentance. This uncompromising stance earned him the reputation of being the "new Elijah."

Victor Gill, in his 2015 papers, argues that John the Baptist embodies the characteristics of Elijah not only in spirit but also in function. John's role was to call out the spiritual complacency and hypocrisy of his generation, urging a return to true worship. He was a voice crying in the wilderness, challenging the status quo and preparing the hearts of the people for the arrival of Jesus. John's ministry was not about pleasing people but about speaking truth to power�a necessary preparation for the Messiah's advent.

John the Baptist's Message: A Call to Repentance

John's message was direct: "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand!" (Matthew 3:2). His call to repentance was not just a plea for moral reformation but a call to recognize the imminent arrival of the Messiah. In this sense, John's ministry was a continuation of Elijah's work�a call to turn away from idolatry and apostasy and return to the true worship of God.

Gill emphasizes that John's message was not merely for the common people but was aimed directly at the religious establishment of his day. The Pharisees and Sadducees, who came to observe his baptisms, were met with a stern rebuke: "You brood of vipers! Who warned you to flee from the wrath to come?" (Matthew 3:7). John did not mince words, nor did he seek to soften his message to avoid offending those in power. His approach was confrontational, exposing the hypocrisy and self-righteousness of the religious leaders while calling them to genuine repentance.

The Confrontational Nature of John's Ministry

Like Elijah, John the Baptist did not shy away from conflict. He challenged King Herod for his unlawful marriage to Herodias, boldly declaring, "It is not lawful for you to have her" (Mark 6:18). This fearless proclamation eventually led to his imprisonment and death. John's ministry was characterized by a willingness to confront sin head-on, regardless of the personal cost.

Walter Veith notes that John�s ministry exemplifies the kind of courage and boldness required in the face of spiritual corruption. Veith argues that John�s death was not in vain but was a necessary act of testimony against the moral decay of his time. In this sense, John�s ministry foreshadowed the experience of the 144,000, who will also face persecution and martyrdom for their unwavering stand for truth.

The Elijah Message in John's Ministry

Gill draws a parallel between the ministries of Elijah and John, noting that both were sent to call God's people back to true worship in times of great apostasy. Elijah confronted the prophets of Baal and challenged the people of Israel to choose whom they would serve. Similarly, John called the people to repentance and baptized them in preparation for the coming Messiah. Both prophets were uncompromising in their message, refusing to accommodate or appease those who were in error.

Stephen Bohr, in his analysis, suggests that the Elijah message is not limited to a single individual but is a recurring theme throughout salvation history. Bohr points out that the spirit of Elijah is a spirit of reformation�a call to return to God's commandments and to worship Him in spirit and truth. John's ministry was an embodiment of this spirit, preparing the way for Jesus by calling the people to repent and be baptized.

The Song of John the Baptist: A Unique Testimony

Revelation 14:3 speaks of a song that "no one else can sing," and this song is often associated with the unique testimony of the 144,000. Gill suggests that John the Baptist also sang a unique song�a song of preparation, repentance, and reformation. His ministry was distinct in that it heralded the first coming of the Messiah, just as the 144,000 will herald His second coming. John's song was one of urgency and warning, a call to make straight the way of the Lord.

John's testimony was unique because it was born out of his personal experience and calling. He lived a life of asceticism in the wilderness, dressed in camel's hair, and subsisted on locusts and wild honey (Matthew 3:4). His lifestyle was a stark contrast to the religious leaders of his day, who lived in luxury and comfort. John's message, like his appearance, was simple, direct, and powerful. It was a song that challenged the established norms and called for a radical return to the basics of faith and obedience to God.

The End-Time Elijah Message: A Call to Decision

Gill emphasizes that the 144,000 will carry a message similar to that of John the Baptist� a call to repentance and preparation for the imminent return of Christ. Just as John's ministry prepared the way for Jesus' first coming, the 144,000 will prepare the world for His second coming. This message will be one of urgency and warning, calling people to make a decisive choice between following God's commandments or aligning themselves with the deceptions of the beast.

Steve Wohlberg adds that the Elijah message is inherently a call to decision. In his writings, he argues that the 144,000 will not be content with half-hearted commitments or superficial conversions. Their message will be a call to total surrender and obedience to God, much like John�s message in the wilderness. Wohlberg suggests that the 144,000 will confront the spiritual leaders of their time, just as John confronted the Pharisees and Sadducees, calling them out for their hypocrisy and spiritual blindness.

John the Baptist's Example for the 144,000

David Asscherick often describes John the Baptist as the prototype for the end-time Elijah message. John's courage, boldness, and unwavering commitment to truth are qualities that will characterize the 144,000. Asscherick points out that John's death at the hands of Herod is a foreshadowing of the persecution that the 144,000 will face in the final crisis. However, like John, they will not be deterred by threats or persecution. They will remain faithful to their calling, proclaiming the message that God has given them, regardless of the cost.

Gill concurs, stating that the 144,000 will be "voices crying in the wilderness" of a spiritually desolate world. Their message will be a call to repentance, a warning of impending judgment, and an invitation to return to true worship. Just as John�s ministry prepared the way for Christ�s first advent, so the ministry of the 144,000 will prepare the world for His return.

Conclusion: The Continuation of the Elijah Message

The Elijah message did not end with Elijah or John the Baptist. It is a message that continues to resonate throughout history, calling God's people to repentance, reformation, and a return to true worship. In the end times, the 144,000 will take up this mantle, proclaiming a message that no one else can sing�a song of judgment, warning, and invitation. They will confront the powers of darkness with an assertive, uncompromising message, much like Elijah and John before them.

As Victor Gill writes, "The Elijah message is a call to stand for truth in a time of great deception, to sing a song that no one else can sing�a song of defiance against the powers of darkness and a declaration of allegiance to the God of heaven."

 

Segment 3: The 144,000 � The Final Elijahs

Introduction: The Identity of the 144,000

The 144,000, mentioned in Revelation 7 and 14, have long been a subject of fascination and debate among theologians and believers. Who are they, and what role will they play in the end times? According to Victor Gill and other scholars, the 144,000 are a special group of people called to carry the Elijah message to the world in the final days of Earth's history. Their mission is to stand as a witness to the power and truth of God amid a world overwhelmed by deception, confusion, and apostasy.

Gill identifies the 144,000 as "spiritual Elijahs," chosen by God to proclaim the three angels' messages of Revelation 14:6-12. These messages warn the world of impending judgment, call for the worship of the Creator, and issue a stern admonition against worshiping the beast and receiving its mark. The 144,000 are unique in that they sing a "new song" (Revelation 14:3) that no one else can sing�a song born out of their experience and their unwavering commitment to God's truth.

The Characteristics of the 144,000

Walter Veith and Stephen Bohr have both emphasized the distinct characteristics of the 144,000. According to Revelation 14:4, they are described as "virgins," which Veith interprets as spiritual purity�those who have not defiled themselves with the "women," symbolic of false doctrines or churches. The 144,000 are also described as those who "follow the Lamb wherever He goes," indicating their complete surrender and obedience to Jesus.

Gill elaborates on these characteristics, suggesting that the 144,000 will have a deep understanding of God's Word and His commandments. They will not be swayed by popular opinion or the false teachings of their time. Instead, they will stand firm on the truth of Scripture, much like Elijah stood firm against the prophets of Baal. They will be people of deep conviction, willing to sacrifice everything for the cause of God.

Steve Wohlberg adds that the 144,000 will be distinguished by their experience. They will have gone through the "great tribulation" (Revelation 7:14), having their faith tested to the utmost. This refining process will make them "without fault before the throne of God" (Revelation 14:5). Wohlberg argues that their experience will be unique because they will have remained faithful to God in the face of the most severe trials and persecutions. This experience will give them a testimony�a "song"�that no one else can sing.

The Role of the 144,000 in the End Times

The 144,000 are not merely passive observers of the final events of Earth's history; they are active participants in God's plan of salvation. Gill notes that they will play a crucial role in proclaiming the three angels' messages, which he identifies as the "final Elijah message." These messages are a call to repentance, a warning of judgment, and an invitation to return to true worship.

David Asscherick points out that the 144,000 will be the final witnesses for God on Earth. They will stand against the beast and its image, refusing to bow to the false system of worship that will be enforced upon the world. Asscherick argues that their mission will be to expose the lies and deceptions of the enemy and to call people to make a decisive choice for or against God. Their message will be bold, confrontational, and unapologetic�much like Elijah's challenge on Mount Carmel.

Doug Batchelor echoes this sentiment, suggesting that the 144,000 will be "spiritual warriors," fighting not with weapons of this world but with the "sword of the Spirit, which is the word of God" (Ephesians 6:17). Batchelor believes that their mission is to confront the powers of darkness with the light of truth, calling people out of Babylon and into a saving relationship with Jesus Christ.

The Song That No One Else Can Sing

The 144,000 are described as singing a song that "no one else can sing" (Revelation 14:3). This song, according to Gill, represents a unique experience and testimony that only they will have. It is a song of victory, born out of their triumph over sin and their steadfast faith in God amid persecution and trial. This song is not just a literal melody; it is a narrative of their journey, their faith, and their commitment to God.

Ty Gibson offers an insightful perspective on the nature of this song. He suggests that the "new song" of the 144,000 is a song of love�love for God and love for humanity. It is a song that reflects their understanding of God's character, His justice, and His mercy. Gibson argues that this song will be a powerful testimony to the world, revealing the true nature of God in contrast to the false portrayals that have been perpetuated by the enemy.

Gill agrees, noting that the song of the 144,000 will be a declaration of their allegiance to God and their rejection of the false systems of worship that have dominated the world. It will be a song of defiance against the beast and its image, a proclamation that they have chosen to "follow the Lamb wherever He goes" (Revelation 14:4). This song will be unique because it will be born out of their experience of standing for truth in a time of great apostasy and deception.

The Elijah Message and the 144,000

Gill emphasizes that the Elijah message is inherently linked to the mission of the 144,000. Just as Elijah stood alone against the prophets of Baal, the 144,000 will stand against the forces of evil in the end times. They will be bold, assertive, and uncompromising in their proclamation of God's truth. Their message will be a call to repentance, a warning of judgment, and an invitation to return to true worship.

Gill quotes Revelation 14:12, which describes the 144,000 as those who "keep the commandments of God and the faith of Jesus." This is the essence of the Elijah message�a call to obedience to God's law and faith in Jesus Christ. The 144,000 will confront the world with a message that demands a decision�either to follow God and His commandments or to follow the beast and receive its mark.

The Assertive Nature of the 144,000's Mission

Like Elijah, the 144,000 will not be "nice" in the conventional sense. They will not seek to appease or accommodate but will speak the truth in love with a boldness that may be perceived as confrontational. Gill argues that this is necessary because the world is in a state of moral and spiritual crisis, and only a clear, distinct message can cut through the confusion and deception.

Joe Crews, in his writings, emphasizes that the 144,000 will be "voices crying in the wilderness" of a world steeped in darkness. Their message will not be comfortable; it will be a "shock to the system," challenging the status quo and calling people to make a decisive stand for or against God. Crews argues that their mission is to confront the powers of darkness with the light of truth, much like Elijah confronted the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel.

David Asscherick concurs, noting that the 144,000 will be "spiritual Elijahs" in the truest sense. They will not be afraid to speak truth to power, to challenge the religious and political authorities of their time, and to call people to repentance and faith in God. Their message will be a call to decision�a choice between life and death, truth and deception, God and the gods of this world.

The Necessity of a Distinctive Message

Gill argues that the distinctiveness of the 144,000's message is crucial. In a world filled with falsehood and deception, the truth must be clear, distinct, and assertive. The 144,000 will not water down their message or seek to make it more palatable for a sinful world. Instead, they will proclaim the truth boldly, regardless of the consequences.

Walter Veith supports this perspective, suggesting that the Elijah message is not meant to be "nice" in the conventional sense but is a necessary "shock to the system." Veith argues that God raises up individuals like Elijah and the 144,000 to confront the powers of darkness with a fearless message of truth. He suggests that this message must be direct, assertive, and unyielding, as it is the last call for humanity to choose whom they will serve.

Conclusion: The Role of the 144,000 in God's Final Plan

The 144,000 are more than just a symbolic number; they represent a group of people uniquely prepared to carry the Elijah message to the world in the final days of Earth's history. They will be spiritual warriors, standing against the forces of evil with a message that is bold, confrontational, and unapologetic. Their mission is to call people to repentance, warn of impending judgment, and invite them to return to true worship.

As Victor Gill concludes in his 2015 papers, "The 144,000 are the final Elijahs, called to sing a song that no one else can sing�a song of defiance against the powers of darkness and a declaration of allegiance to the God of heaven. They are the voices crying in the wilderness, calling humanity to make a choice between life and death, truth and deception, God and the gods of this world."

Segment 4: The Three Angels� Messages � The Core of the Elijah Message

Introduction: The Urgency of the Three Angels' Messages

The Three Angels' Messages, found in Revelation 14:6-12, form the core of the Elijah message in the end times. These messages are a call to repentance, a warning of impending judgment, and an invitation to worship the Creator. Victor Gill, in his 2015 papers, emphasizes that these messages are not optional; they are a divine mandate given to the 144,000 to proclaim to the entire world before the return of Jesus Christ. Just as Elijah called Israel back to true worship and John the Baptist prepared the way for the Messiah, the 144,000 will deliver these three angels' messages with urgency, clarity, and boldness.

The First Angel's Message: A Call to Worship the Creator

The first angel's message declares, "Fear God and give glory to Him, for the hour of His judgment has come; and worship Him who made heaven and earth, the sea and springs of water" (Revelation 14:7). This message is a call to recognize God as the Creator and to worship Him alone. Gill argues that this is the foundational message of the Elijah message�a call to true worship. In a world filled with idolatry, materialism, and secularism, the first angel's message calls people back to the worship of the one true God.

Walter Veith emphasizes that the command to "fear God" is not about being afraid but about reverence, awe, and obedience. To "give glory to Him" means to reflect His character in our lives, to honor Him in all that we do, and to recognize His authority over our lives. Veith argues that this message is especially relevant today, as many have lost sight of the Creator and have turned to worship the creation instead. The call to "worship Him who made heaven and earth" is a call to return to the Sabbath, the sign of God's creative power and authority.

Stephen Bohr expands on this by highlighting the Sabbath as a key component of the first angel's message. He points out that the Sabbath commandment, found in Exodus 20:8-11, is a memorial of creation and a sign of God's authority. By keeping the Sabbath, God's people acknowledge Him as the Creator and demonstrate their allegiance to Him. Bohr argues that the Sabbath will be a test of loyalty in the end times, as the world is called to worship the Creator on His holy day.

The Second Angel's Message: A Warning Against Babylon

The second angel's message declares, "Babylon is fallen, is fallen, that great city, because she has made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication" (Revelation 14:8). This message is a warning against spiritual Babylon�a symbol of false religion, apostasy, and confusion. Gill points out that just as Elijah confronted the prophets of Baal, who led Israel into idolatry, the 144,000 will confront spiritual Babylon, exposing its errors and calling people to come out of it.

Steve Wohlberg explains that Babylon represents a system of false worship and deception that has led the world away from true worship. He suggests that the "wine of the wrath of her fornication" represents the false doctrines and teachings that have intoxicated the nations. Wohlberg argues that the second angel's message is a call to separate from all forms of apostasy and to reject the false teachings that have corrupted Christianity.

David Asscherick adds that the fall of Babylon is both a historical and a prophetic reality. Babylon fell historically when the literal city was conquered by the Medes and Persians, but its spiritual counterpart�symbolized by false systems of worship�will fall in the end times when its deceptions are exposed, and people are called out of her. Asscherick emphasizes that the call to "come out of her, my people" (Revelation 18:4) is a call to leave behind all forms of error and false worship and to align oneself with the truth of God's Word.

The Third Angel's Message: A Dire Warning Against the Mark of the Beast

The third angel's message is the most solemn and severe: "If anyone worships the beast and his image, and receives his mark on his forehead or on his hand, he himself shall also drink of the wine of the wrath of God" (Revelation 14:9-10). This message warns against receiving the mark of the beast and participating in false worship. Gill argues that this message is the final appeal to humanity�a call to choose between the true worship of God and the false worship promoted by the beast.

Ty Gibson points out that the "beast" and its "image" represent false systems of worship that stand in opposition to God's commandments. He suggests that the "mark of the beast" is not a literal mark but a symbol of allegiance to these false systems. Gibson emphasizes that the third angel's message is a call to remain faithful to God, even in the face of persecution and coercion. It is a call to stand firm in one's convictions and to refuse to compromise one's faith.

Doug Batchelor adds that the third angel's message is a call to total commitment to God. He argues that those who receive the mark of the beast do so because they have compromised their faith to avoid conflict, persecution, or hardship. Batchelor suggests that the 144,000 will be those who refuse to compromise, who stand firm in their loyalty to God, and who are willing to endure whatever trials may come for the sake of their faith.

The Three Angels' Messages as the Final Elijah Message

Gill emphasizes that the Three Angels' Messages are the final Elijah message to the world. Just as Elijah confronted the idolatry and apostasy of his time, the 144,000 will confront the false systems of worship that have deceived the world. Their message will be a call to repentance, a warning of impending judgment, and an invitation to worship the Creator. It will be a message that demands a decision�either to follow God and keep His commandments or to follow the beast and receive its mark.

Stephen Bohr argues that the Three Angels' Messages are a continuation of the Elijah message that began with Elijah on Mount Carmel and continued with John the Baptist in the wilderness. He points out that these messages are not only a call to worship but also a call to obedience. Bohr suggests that the true test in the end times will be obedience to God's commandments, particularly the Sabbath, which is a sign of God's authority as Creator.

The Urgency and Assertiveness of the Three Angels' Messages

The Three Angels' Messages are not a "soft" or "gentle" call; they are an urgent, assertive proclamation that demands a response. Joe Crews emphasizes that these messages are a "shock to the system," designed to wake people up from their spiritual slumber and call them to a decision. Crews argues that in a time of great deception and apostasy, the truth must be proclaimed boldly and without compromise.

Gill concurs, noting that the 144,000 will not seek to make their message palatable to a sinful world. Their mission is not to please people but to call them to repentance and faithfulness to God. Gill argues that the Three Angels' Messages are inherently confrontational because they challenge the very foundations of false worship and call people to make a clear, unambiguous choice.

The Relevance of the Three Angels' Messages Today

In today's world, where confusion, deception, and false worship abound, the Three Angels' Messages are more relevant than ever. Gill emphasizes that these messages are not just for a future time; they are for today. The call to "fear God and give glory to Him" is a call to live a life that reflects God's character, to keep His commandments, and to honor Him in all things. The warning against Babylon is a call to separate from all forms of error and falsehood and to align oneself with the truth of God's Word. The warning against the mark of the beast is a call to remain faithful to God, even in the face of persecution and trial.

Walter Veith suggests that the Three Angels' Messages are a blueprint for end-time living. They provide a clear, distinct message that cuts through the confusion and deception of our time. Veith argues that these messages call for a total commitment to God, a willingness to stand for truth, and a readiness to face whatever trials may come.

Conclusion: The Elijah Message in the Final Crisis

The Three Angels' Messages are the final Elijah message to the world. They are a call to repentance, a warning of judgment, and an invitation to worship the Creator. They are a message that demands a decision�a choice between life and death, truth and deception, God and the gods of this world. The 144,000, as the final Elijahs, will proclaim these messages with urgency, clarity, and boldness, calling people to make a decisive stand for or against God.

As Victor Gill concludes in his 2015 papers, "The Three Angels' Messages are the last call of mercy to a dying world. They are a call to come out of Babylon, to worship the Creator, and to remain faithful to God in a time of great apostasy and deception. They are the final Elijah message, and they must be proclaimed with urgency, assertiveness, and a clear call to decision."

 

Segment 5: The Modern Application of the Elijah Message

Introduction: The Elijah Message in Contemporary Context

The Elijah message, as revealed through the stories of Elijah, John the Baptist, and the 144,000, is a call for decisive action in a world increasingly characterized by moral relativism, spiritual confusion, and outright apostasy. This message has never been more relevant than it is today. In a time when truth is often obscured by a cloud of deception and compromise, the Elijah message calls for a return to the pure worship of God, a stand for righteousness, and a rejection of falsehood in all its forms.

Victor Gill, in his 2015 papers, emphasizes that the Elijah message is not just a relic of biblical history but a living, breathing mandate for all believers. Gill suggests that every Christian today is called to embody the spirit of Elijah by standing firm for truth, proclaiming the gospel, and preparing the world for Christ's soon return. This is not a time for passivity or compromise; it is a time for boldness, courage, and conviction.

The Elijah Message and the Crisis of Truth

Today, we find ourselves in a cultural and spiritual crisis. Truth is under attack on multiple fronts�politically, socially, and even within the religious world. The rise of relativism has led to a rejection of absolute truth, where personal opinion often takes precedence over divine revelation. In such a context, the Elijah message is a call to return to the clear, unchanging truths of God's Word.

Walter Veith argues that the crisis of truth today mirrors the spiritual crisis in Elijah's time. Just as Israel wavered between worshiping Yahweh and the false gods of their neighbors, many today waver between following God and conforming to the ideologies of the world. Veith suggests that the Elijah message is a call to "stop wavering" and make a clear decision: "If the Lord is God, follow Him; but if Baal, follow him" (1 Kings 18:21).

Steve Wohlberg concurs, noting that the Elijah message is inherently confrontational because it challenges the prevailing norms and calls for a radical return to God's commandments. He argues that in a world where compromise is often seen as a virtue, the Elijah message demands a stand for truth, even when it is unpopular or politically incorrect.

The Elijah Message and the Church

The Elijah message is not only for the world at large but also for the Church itself. Victor Gill points out that one of the greatest challenges facing the Church today is the infiltration of false teachings, doctrines, and practices that undermine the pure gospel of Jesus Christ. The Elijah message calls for reformation and revival within the Church, a return to biblical principles, and a rejection of any doctrine or practice that is not grounded in Scripture.

Stephen Bohr suggests that just as Elijah challenged the religious leaders of his time, the modern Elijah message must also challenge the spiritual complacency, hypocrisy, and corruption that exist within the Church. Bohr argues that the Church must return to the "faith once delivered to the saints" (Jude 1:3) and reject any teaching or practice that is contrary to the clear teaching of Scripture.

Ty Gibson expands on this by emphasizing the need for a spirit of humility and repentance within the Church. He notes that the Elijah message is not a call to self-righteousness or condemnation but a call to recognize our own need for grace, repentance, and transformation. Gibson suggests that the Church must first experience a revival of true godliness before it can effectively proclaim the Elijah message to the world.

The Elijah Message and the Individual Believer

The Elijah message is not only a corporate call but also a personal one. Every believer is called to embody the spirit of Elijah by standing firm for truth, living a life of holiness, and proclaiming the gospel with boldness. Victor Gill emphasizes that the Elijah message is a call to personal integrity, obedience, and faithfulness in a world that often pressures believers to conform to its values.

David Asscherick notes that the story of Elijah is a reminder that God often works through a faithful remnant, those who are willing to stand for truth even when it seems they are standing alone. Asscherick argues that each believer today must decide whether they will be part of that faithful remnant, proclaiming the Elijah message in their own sphere of influence.

Doug Batchelor adds that the Elijah message is a call to courage. He argues that it takes courage to stand for truth in a world that often ridicules or persecutes those who do. Batchelor suggests that believers must be willing to face opposition, criticism, and even persecution for the sake of the gospel. He points to Elijah, John the Baptist, and the 144,000 as examples of those who stood firm in their convictions despite immense pressure to conform or compromise.

The Elijah Message and Evangelism

One of the most critical applications of the Elijah message today is in the area of evangelism. The message of repentance, reformation, and return to true worship must be proclaimed to all nations, just as the Three Angels' Messages are to be proclaimed to "every nation, tribe, tongue, and people" (Revelation 14:6). Victor Gill argues that evangelism is at the heart of the Elijah message, and every believer is called to share this message with those around them.

Joe Crews highlights the importance of boldness in evangelism. He suggests that the Elijah message requires a fearless proclamation of the truth, without compromise or apology. Crews argues that the time for "soft" or "safe" evangelism is over; the world needs to hear a clear, distinct call to repentance and faith in Jesus Christ.

Stephen Bohr expands on this by emphasizing the need for a balanced approach to evangelism. He argues that while the message must be bold and assertive, it must also be delivered with love and compassion. Bohr suggests that the Elijah message is not about condemning people but about calling them to the grace, mercy, and truth of God. He points to the example of Jesus, who was both bold and compassionate in His ministry.

The Elijah Message and the Prophetic Movement

The Elijah message is also closely tied to the prophetic movement within Christianity. Victor Gill notes that just as Elijah, John the Baptist, and the 144,000 are all prophetic figures, so too is the Church called to be a prophetic voice in the world today. This involves not only proclaiming the truths of Scripture but also warning of the deceptions, dangers, and judgments to come.

Walter Veith argues that the Elijah message must include a prophetic element, warning people of the signs of the times and the nearness of Christ's return. He suggests that the Church must be vigilant in identifying and exposing false teachings, doctrines, and movements that lead people away from the truth.

Doug Batchelor adds that the Elijah message is a call to prepare for the final events of Earth's history. He argues that just as Elijah prepared Israel for a decisive moment on Mount Carmel, and John the Baptist prepared the people for the coming of the Messiah, so too must the Church prepare the world for Christ's return. Batchelor suggests that this involves both a proclamation of the gospel and a warning of the judgments to come.

The Elijah Message and the Spirit of Prophecy

The Elijah message is also closely tied to the Spirit of Prophecy, which, according to Revelation 19:10, is the "testimony of Jesus." Victor Gill argues that the Spirit of Prophecy is an essential component of the Elijah message, providing guidance, direction, and understanding of God's will for His people in the end times.

Ty Gibson points out that the Spirit of Prophecy is not about predicting the future or setting dates but about understanding the times in which we live and knowing how to respond in faithfulness to God. Gibson suggests that the Elijah message involves a deep understanding of the prophecies of Scripture and a willingness to follow God's leading, even when it is difficult or unpopular.

David Asscherick adds that the Spirit of Prophecy is also about calling people to a deeper relationship with Jesus Christ. He argues that the Elijah message is ultimately a call to love God with all our hearts, to follow Him fully, and to live lives that reflect His character. Asscherick suggests that the Spirit of Prophecy is a gift that helps the Church understand God's will and prepare for the final events of Earth's history.

The Elijah Message and the End-Time Generation

The Elijah message is ultimately a message for the end-time generation�a call to stand firm for truth in a time of great apostasy, deception, and crisis. Victor Gill emphasizes that the 144,000 are the final Elijahs, called to proclaim the Three Angels' Messages with urgency, clarity, and boldness. They will be spiritual warriors, standing against the forces of evil with a message that is both confrontational and compassionate.

Stephen Bohr suggests that the end-time generation is called to be like Elijah, John the Baptist, and the 144,000�bold, courageous, and uncompromising in their proclamation of the gospel. He argues that this generation must be willing to face persecution, hardship, and even death for the sake of the truth.

Doug Batchelor concurs, noting that the end-time generation will face unprecedented challenges but will also have unprecedented opportunities to witness for Christ. He suggests that the Elijah message is a call to prepare, to be vigilant, and to be ready to stand for truth no matter the cost.

Conclusion: The Elijah Message for Today

The Elijah message is a call for our time�a call to repentance, reformation, and a return to true worship. It is a call to stand for truth in a world filled with deception, to be bold in proclaiming the gospel, and to prepare for the soon return of Jesus Christ. The 144,000, as the final Elijahs, will proclaim this message with urgency, clarity, and conviction, calling all people to make a choice between life and death, truth and deception, God and the gods of this world.

As Victor Gill concludes in his 2015 papers, "The Elijah message is a call to stand firm for God in a time of great crisis. It is a call to be bold, courageous, and uncompromising in our proclamation of the gospel. It is a call to prepare the world for the soon return of Jesus Christ."
